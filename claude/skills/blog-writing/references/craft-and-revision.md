# Craft moves and the revision loop

## Craft moves

The imitable techniques behind the register. Each is a move you can deploy, not a vibe.

- **Coin a handle.** Give the core idea a name, define it once, reuse it. It makes the piece coherent and quotable. *ML:* "Call it eval leakage: when your benchmark quietly contains its own training data."
- **Dated vignettes.** Open with time-stamped scenes and let evidence pile up before you name the pattern. *Startups:* "2019: eleven customers, and we called it traction. 2021: eleven thousand, and it felt the same."
- **The one-sentence-paragraph beat.** After a buildup, drop a single short sentence on its own line. It lands harder than any bold or italics. *Bio:* a paragraph on a year of failed assays, then: "The control was contaminated the whole time."
- **Self-deprecation as evidence.** Quote your own past overconfidence, then show what actually happened; the gap is the argument. Put yourself inside the lesson. Use a sentence break for the reveal, never parentheses, which would bury it. *Startups:* "My deck said we were 'the AWS of biology.' We had one customer. He was my roommate."
- **Inoculate, then argue.** State the strongest version of the opposing view first, with specifics and names, before showing where it breaks. The reader who would object has been disarmed.
- **Scare-quotes to own a loaded word.** Signal you know a word is contestable and are using it on purpose. *Startups:* Drew "wasted" five years on products we pivoted away from.
- **Contrast in direct speech.** Dramatize a distinction as two quoted thoughts side by side, so the reader feels the difference rather than being told it. "Mentor X thinks this is bad" versus "the world works differently than you expected."
- **Quantify the anecdote.** Attach real numbers (a range where a point estimate would overclaim). "Forty employee-years" is what turns a story into evidence.
- **Anaphora.** Repeat an opening phrase across sentences to knit them and build rhythm. Use sparingly, at a moment that earns the drum.
- **The confidence dial.** "I think" is fine when it calibrates ("I think, maybe 60%, that..."). Cut it when it is only nerves.
- **One institutional or historical example.** A single outside precedent shows the idea is not idiosyncratic. *Abyss:* the Quaker clearness committee, in one sentence, makes "stare into the abyss" a perennial human practice rather than one blogger's hobbyhorse.

## The loose-then-tight loop

PG's actual process, and the one to run: loose first, then tight. They fight each other, so do not mix them.

**Loose (drafting).** Write the bad version fast. Try many things. Chase tangents (one might be the real essay). Do not polish while generating. Stop a session while you still know what comes next, so restarting is easy.

**Tight (revising).** This is where most of the value is.

1. **Read it aloud** (or have the user). Two signals surface that the eye misses: where you stumble is awkward phrasing, and where you are bored is dead weight. If the user dreads reading a paragraph, every reader will skip it. Cut it.
2. **Chase what catches.** Reread the key passages until nothing snags. A snag is usually a wrong *idea*, not just a rough phrase ("Ugh, this does not sound right; what do I mean to say here?"), so fix the thinking, not only the words. Writing that sounds right is more likely to *be* right ([Good Writing](https://paulgraham.com/goodwriting.html)).
3. **The two-question test** (Nielsen). Of each sentence, ask both: can it be *sharper*, and do I *truly believe* this? The first is craft, the second is rigor; a sentence that fails either is not done.
4. **Cut.** Shorter is almost always better; be confident enough to cut. For each sentence: is the post worse without it? Keep surprises (a surprise may become the spine); send digressions to a footnote.

## Danger words

Words that almost always signal a problem (Nielsen):

- **"interesting" / "important."** Do not announce that something is interesting; that instructs the reader how to feel and quietly admits the prose is not doing it. Delete the announcement and make the next sentence interesting.
- **"very" / "quite."** Generic intensifiers. Replace with the specific word: not "very hungry" but "ravenous"; not "very fast growth" but the number.
- **The throat-clearing openers.** "It's worth noting," "It's interesting that," "In this post I will." Cut and start on the first true sentence.

## Classic vs personal style

A subtle one that matters for a writer working across fields of uneven expertise. The confident, authoritative "classic" style implies mastery. Where the user genuinely is the expert (their core ML or biosecurity work), it fits. Where they are newer to a subject, an exploratory **personal** style, one that shows the reasoning and the limits of what they know, is both more honest and more readable than borrowed authority (Nielsen). Boldness of *style* should track mastery of *subject*. This is how "be bold" and "be calibrated" stop fighting: be bold where you have earned it, exploratory where you have not.
