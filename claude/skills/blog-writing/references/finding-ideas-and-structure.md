## Finding an idea

An essay is something the user writes to figure something out; if they already knew the conclusion cold, it is a report, not an essay. The aim is to surprise themselves first ([The Shape of the Essay Field](https://paulgraham.com/field.html)). Run these tests; each is close to a yes/no the user can answer fast.

- **Said it twice.** Has the user explained or argued this more than once in real conversation? ([Ben Kuhn](https://www.benkuhn.net/writing/)). The most operational test there is: if yes, the draft already exists in their head.
- **Does it surprise the user, after real thought?** Novelty to a writer who has thought hard predicts novelty to readers. The sweet spot is an idea that feels *simultaneously novel and obvious* ([How to Do Great Work](https://paulgraham.com/greatwork.html)).
- **What does the field get wrong?** If the user is excited about something everyone else ignores *and* can say precisely what they are all overlooking, that is as good a bet as exists. Precision is the whole value; "they're missing something" is not a post, "they're conflating threat-model A with threat-model B" is.
- **What did the user learn late that matters?** "Which important things do people tend to learn late?" is an essay generator on its own.
- **Where does the conventional explanation fail?** There is already a standard story about transformers, about scaling, about deterrence. Restating it clearly is not enough (Michael Nielsen, [Notes on Writing](https://github.com/mnielsen/notes-on-writing/blob/master/notes_on_writing.md)). The post is the angle that goes past it; striking prose is a byproduct of seeing something fresh, not the goal.

Reliable post shapes when nothing jumps out (Kuhn): explain a concept the user keeps explaining; document a project they built; review a book; a curated link roundup; a rant; or an argument against a common claim that is wrong.

## Why publish the imperfect one

The user selected being pushed and writing across three fields, so the failure mode to fight is not publishing a mediocre post, it is publishing nothing. The case for shipping:

- **Posts are outliers.** The best are massively better than the average, so the move is volume and shots on goal: write a lot, watch what resonates ([Ben Kuhn](https://www.benkuhn.net/writing/)). The bar to *create value* is lower than the user thinks; the bar to *publish* (the bar in `SKILL.md`) is what stays high.
- **The payoff is friendships, not fame.** A large share of these writers' close relationships came through their blogs. That reframes "is this good enough for strangers" into "would I send this to a friend who cares."
- **Write what you are most excited about** right now, not what you think you should write, not what you would be most excited to *have* written. Excitement is what produces consistent output, and consistency compounds: a page a day is a book a year. Publishing on a cadence also makes the user write more, which generates more ideas.

Picking among candidates: rank by excitement, filtered through the bar. Narrow until the user is the expert on that specific angle; ten readers who genuinely care is a fine target. Breadth comes later, as the body of work accumulates.

## Shaping the arc

**One sharp purpose.** The user should be able to answer "what is this about?" in one compelling sentence. If they cannot, the post has no single purpose and will read as "sort of about gardens and sort of about rainforests, and not really about anything" (Nielsen). Write that sentence at the top of the draft file. Then cut everything that does not serve it, no matter how good; cut material goes to a scrap file, not the trash (today's digression is next month's post).

**Structural patterns worth reaching for** (default is open: lead with the point; delay only to disarm a counterintuitive claim, per the register):

- **Define, then two examples.** Name the concept in one plain sentence, then ground it in two examples that are as different as possible, so the reader sees its range ([abyss](https://www.benkuhn.net/abyss/) opens this way).
- **Dated vignettes before the thesis.** Open with time-stamped scenes and let evidence accumulate before naming the pattern; earns a claim the reader would reject if stated cold ([hard](https://www.benkuhn.net/hard/)).
- **Diagnosis before prescription.** Explain *why* the mistake happens before telling the reader what to do. The reader discovers the conclusion instead of being handed it.
- **Name the concept early, use it throughout** ([conviction](https://www.benkuhn.net/conviction/)). Coining a handle ("staring into the abyss") makes the piece coherent and quotable.
- **Inoculate, then argue.** Grant the strongest version of the opposing view first, with specifics, before showing where it fails. Readers who would resist have been respected, so they stay.
- **Front-load why it matters.** One paragraph on stakes before diving into the technical content; do not assume the reader already cares.

## Openings and the title

**The opening sentence** does two jobs at once: be striking enough to earn the next sentence, and start as late as possible (cut the runway). For a post that matters, brainstorm ten or more openings before committing; an hour here is not wasted. When blocked, Hemingway's move is to "write one true sentence" and go from there.

**The title is the highest-leverage sentence in the post**, the one edit with the largest effect on whether anyone reads it. It promises an exciting, accurate takeaway, not a topic label. The strongest name the central concept ("The Selfish Gene") or the problem; surprising-but-apt juxtaposition works. "Why most AI biosecurity risk framings are confused" beats "Thoughts on biosecurity and AI."

## Endings

End by enacting the thesis, not summarizing it: turn it on the reader, issue the imperative, or open the next question (Kuhn closes [conviction](https://www.benkuhn.net/conviction/) on "So get out there and make some!"; PG often ends by opening a question). Learn to recognize the approach of an ending and grab it; do not write past it. For drafting momentum across sessions, stop while you still know what comes next.

Two structural homes for material that does not belong in the spine: **footnotes** hold digressions (distinguish a surprise, which may *be* the essay, from a digression, which is a footnote or a cut). And keep defenses against deliberate misreading in endnotes, not the main argument: do not put bars on the windows of the essay ([How to Write Usefully](https://paulgraham.com/useful.html)).
